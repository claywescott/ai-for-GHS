Module 4: AI Applications in Global Health Security

Learning Objectives

  • Understand key applications of AI in global health security
  • Identify specific use cases where AI can enhance disease surveillance and outbreak response
  • Recognize the potential and limitations of AI in health emergency management
  • Apply AI tools to address real-world health security challenges

Why AI Matters for Global Health Security

Artificial intelligence is transforming how we approach global health security challenges by enhancing our ability to detect, predict, prepare for, and respond to health threats. Here's why AI is becoming increasingly important in this field:

Data Processing at Scale

AI can analyze vast amounts of health data from diverse sources (clinical reports, social media, environmental monitoring) far more rapidly than traditional methods, enabling early detection of potential outbreaks.

Pattern Recognition

AI excels at identifying subtle patterns and anomalies in data that might indicate emerging health threats before they become obvious through traditional surveillance methods.

Predictive Capabilities

AI models can forecast disease spread patterns, helping health authorities allocate resources more effectively and implement targeted interventions.

Accessibility and Efficiency

AI tools can make specialized health security knowledge more accessible to regions with limited resources or expertise, democratizing access to critical information.

Key Applications of AI in Global Health Security

1. Disease Surveillance and Early Warning Systems

AI systems can monitor and analyze data from multiple sources to detect unusual patterns that might indicate an emerging outbreak, potentially days or weeks before traditional surveillance methods.

Examples:

  • Natural language processing to analyze social media posts for unusual symptom reports
  • Machine learning algorithms that detect anomalies in hospital admission data
  • AI systems monitoring environmental factors known to correlate with specific disease outbreaks
2. Outbreak Analytics and Modeling

AI can create sophisticated models to predict how diseases might spread, helping public health officials make informed decisions about resource allocation and intervention strategies.

Examples:

  • Predictive models of disease transmission based on population movement data
  • Simulation of different intervention scenarios to identify optimal strategies
  • Real-time adjustment of epidemiological models as new data becomes available
3. Rapid Information Synthesis and Knowledge Management

During health emergencies, AI can quickly synthesize and summarize vast amounts of scientific literature, reports, and guidelines to provide decision-makers with the most relevant, up-to-date information.

Examples:

  • Summarizing new research findings on emerging pathogens
  • Creating customized briefings on specific aspects of outbreak response
  • Comparing protocols and guidelines across different jurisdictions or organizations
4. Community Education and Communication

AI can help develop and tailor risk communication strategies for different audiences, ensuring that critical health information is accessible, culturally appropriate, and effectively delivered.

Examples:

  • Generating educational materials tailored to specific communities or demographics
  • Creating message templates for different scenarios that can be rapidly deployed
  • Analyzing communication effectiveness and suggesting improvements
5. Resource Optimization and Logistics

During health emergencies, AI can help optimize the allocation of limited resources (vaccines, medications, medical staff) to maximize impact and minimize mortality.

Examples:

  • Predicting areas that will need additional medical supplies or personnel
  • Optimizing vaccine distribution strategies based on population risk factors
  • Managing supply chains and tracking resources during emergency responses

Case Studies: AI in Action

Case Study 1: AI in the Mpox Multi-Country Outbreak Response (2022-2025)

Background: Beginning in May 2022, mpox (formerly monkeypox) spread to over 100 countries in an unprecedented multi-country outbreak. WHO declared it a Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC) in July 2022. The outbreak required rapid adaptation of surveillance systems, vaccine distribution strategies, and community engagement approaches.

The Challenge:

  • Traditional surveillance systems were not designed for this transmission pattern
  • Limited global vaccine supply needed equitable allocation
  • Stigma and misinformation complicated public health messaging
  • Rapidly evolving clinical and epidemiological understanding
  • Need for coordinated international response despite varying national capacities

How AI Tools Were Applied:

1. Surveillance Enhancement

  • Social Media Monitoring: AI-powered tools analyzed social media and online forums to detect early signals of unusual symptoms and identify affected communities
  • Pattern Recognition: Machine learning algorithms identified clusters and transmission patterns that weren't immediately obvious in traditional case reports
  • Data Integration: AI helped integrate data from multiple sources (clinical, laboratory, epidemiological) to provide real-time situational awareness

2. Risk Communication

  • Message Tailoring: AI assisted in creating culturally appropriate, community-specific health messages to address stigma while promoting protective behaviors
  • Misinformation Detection: Natural language processing identified and tracked misinformation themes, enabling targeted counter-messaging
  • Multi-language Content: AI translation tools helped rapidly adapt messaging for diverse linguistic communities

3. Resource Allocation

  • Vaccine Distribution Modeling: Predictive models helped optimize limited vaccine supply allocation based on transmission patterns and population vulnerability
  • Healthcare Capacity Planning: AI analyzed outbreak trends to predict healthcare resource needs in different regions

4. Knowledge Synthesis

  • Literature Review: AI tools rapidly synthesized emerging research on mpox clinical presentation, transmission, and treatment
  • Guidance Updates: AI helped draft updated clinical guidance as new evidence emerged
  • Training Materials: Generated educational content for healthcare workers adapting to this "new" pathogen in their settings

Key Lessons for AI Use in Health Security:

  1. Adaptability is Crucial: Surveillance systems must be flexible enough to detect unexpected transmission patterns. AI can help adapt case definitions and detection algorithms quickly.
  2. Community Engagement Matters: AI-generated content must be reviewed by community representatives to ensure cultural appropriateness and avoid perpetuating stigma.
  3. Human Oversight Essential: While AI can process vast amounts of data quickly, human epidemiologists must validate findings and make final decisions.
  4. Equity Considerations: AI tools for resource allocation must be explicitly designed to promote equity, not reinforce existing disparities.
  5. Real-time Learning: AI can help synthesize emerging evidence rapidly, but outputs must be clearly labeled with confidence levels and caveats.

Practical Application for GCSP Course Participants:

Try This Exercise:

Use an AI tool to analyze the mpox response. Ask it: "What were the three most critical gaps in the international mpox outbreak response (2022-2025), and what specific improvements should be made for future similar outbreaks? Focus on surveillance, vaccine equity, and stigma management."

Compare the AI's response with:

  • WHO's mpox outbreak updates and lessons learned reports
  • Published analyses in journals like The Lancet
  • Your own country's or region's experience

This exercise helps you understand both the capabilities and limitations of AI for outbreak analysis.

Case Study 2: Early Warning of the COVID-19 Outbreak (Historical Context)

In late December 2019, the AI-powered disease surveillance system BlueDot detected unusual pneumonia cases in Wuhan, China, and issued an alert to its clients about a potential outbreak. This warning came several days before official notifications from the WHO or national health agencies.

Key Insight: AI systems that scan diverse information sources (news reports, airline ticketing data, animal disease networks) can identify potential outbreaks earlier than traditional surveillance methods. However, this case also highlighted the need for human verification and the importance of official reporting channels.

Practical Applications for Health Security Professionals

5. Laboratory Network Coordination and Diagnostics

Laboratory networks are critical infrastructure for global health security, enabling early detection, pathogen characterization, and outbreak response. AI can enhance coordination, quality assurance, and data sharing across these networks.

Key Applications:

  • Network Coordination: AI can help optimize sample routing, manage referral systems, and coordinate between national, regional, and WHO reference laboratories
  • Quality Assurance Programs: Machine learning algorithms can identify patterns in external quality assessment (EQA) data to target training and support
  • Diagnostic Algorithm Development: AI can help design optimal testing algorithms based on disease prevalence, test characteristics, and resource availability
  • Data Integration: Natural language processing can extract and standardize information from diverse laboratory reports for surveillance purposes
  • Capacity Gap Analysis: AI can analyze laboratory network data to identify geographic or technical gaps in diagnostic coverage
  • Outbreak Support: Predictive models can anticipate laboratory capacity needs during outbreaks and optimize resource deployment

Practical Examples from GCSP Course Context:

Example 1: Supporting the CoViNet Laboratory Network

WHO's COVID-19 laboratory network (CoViNet) coordinates hundreds of laboratories globally. AI applications include:

  • Analyzing variant surveillance data to identify concerning mutations
  • Predicting which variants may escape vaccine immunity
  • Optimizing sequencing strategies based on epidemiological patterns
  • Generating network performance reports from diverse data sources

Example 2: Mpox Laboratory Diagnostics Expansion

During the 2022-2025 mpox outbreak, rapid diagnostic expansion was needed. AI helped by:

  • Identifying laboratories with existing capacity that could be adapted for mpox testing
  • Generating training materials for new testing sites
  • Analyzing testing patterns to optimize test kit distribution
  • Synthesizing emerging guidance on diagnostic approaches

Example 3: Polio Laboratory Network

The Global Polio Laboratory Network uses AI for:

  • Analyzing environmental surveillance data to detect poliovirus circulation
  • Optimizing sampling strategies for maximum detection sensitivity
  • Coordinating international shipping of samples to reference labs
  • Tracking laboratory performance metrics across 140+ laboratories
6. Biorisk Management and Laboratory Biosafety

As discussed in the GCSP course module on biorisk management, ensuring safe and secure handling of dangerous pathogens is fundamental to global health security. AI can support biorisk assessment and management practices.

Applications:

  • Risk Assessment: AI can help analyze the risk profile of research involving dangerous pathogens by considering multiple factors (pathogen characteristics, laboratory capabilities, local context)
  • Incident Analysis: Machine learning can identify patterns in laboratory incident reports to prevent future occurrences
  • Training and Competency: AI can help develop personalized training programs based on staff roles and identified competency gaps
  • Regulatory Compliance: AI tools can help laboratories track compliance with biosafety regulations and standards
  • Protocol Development: AI can assist in drafting standard operating procedures based on international best practices

Important Caveat: While AI can support biorisk management, final decisions about handling dangerous pathogens must always involve expert human judgment, ethical review, and adherence to national and international regulations.

AI for Global Health Security in the Current Geopolitical Context (2026)

Navigating a Changed International Landscape:

The global health security environment of 2026 differs significantly from previous years. Changes in international cooperation frameworks, including shifts in WHO participation and funding, affect how AI tools can and should be used for global health security work.

Key Contextual Factors:

  • Evolving International Frameworks: The 2025 WHO Pandemic Agreement and 2024 IHR amendments establish new obligations, but implementation varies across countries
  • Fragmented Cooperation: Some countries have different levels of engagement with international health mechanisms, affecting data sharing and coordination
  • Regional Approaches: Increased emphasis on regional health security mechanisms (Africa CDC, EU health security, etc.) alongside global frameworks
  • Technology Sovereignty: Growing concerns about data sovereignty and the geographic location of AI service providers
  • Disinformation Challenges: Increased spread of health disinformation complicates outbreak response and requires careful AI use for risk communication

Practical Implications for AI Use in Health Security

Opportunities

  • Bridging Gaps: AI can help synthesize information from multiple sources when official channels are limited
  • Local Capacity: AI tools democratize access to analytical capabilities for resource-limited settings
  • Rapid Adaptation: AI enables quick scenario planning and response adaptation in uncertain environments
  • Evidence Synthesis: AI helps navigate conflicting information by analyzing multiple sources
  • Regional Collaboration: AI facilitates information sharing within regional health security networks

Challenges to Navigate

  • Data Access: Limited access to some international databases or surveillance systems
  • Trust and Verification: Need for multiple verification sources when usual channels are disrupted
  • Bias Risks: AI trained on historical data may not reflect current geopolitical realities
  • Platform Dependencies: Over-reliance on tools from any single country or region creates vulnerabilities
  • Misinformation: AI can both detect and inadvertently spread health misinformation
Practical Guidance for 2026 Context

When Using AI for International Health Security Work:

1. Diversify Your Sources

  • Use multiple AI platforms from different providers
  • Cross-reference AI outputs with official WHO, regional, and national sources
  • Consult peer-reviewed literature and not just AI-generated summaries

2. Be Explicit About Limitations

  • When presenting AI-generated analysis, clearly state it as such
  • Acknowledge where information may be incomplete due to limited access
  • Note the AI's knowledge cutoff date (typically early 2026 for current models)

3. Verify Sensitive Information

  • For policy-relevant information, always verify against official sources
  • Be especially careful with information about:
    • International agreements and their ratification status
    • Country participation in global health mechanisms
    • Funding commitments and financial arrangements
    • Official positions of governments or organizations

4. Consider Data Sovereignty

  • Be mindful of where your data is processed and stored
  • For highly sensitive work, consider locally-deployed AI models
  • Understand your organization's policies on cross-border data flows

5. Focus on What AI Does Best

  • Use AI for synthesis and analysis, not as a primary source
  • Leverage AI for scenario planning and "what-if" analyses
  • Apply AI to identify patterns in complex data
  • Use AI to generate draft materials that humans then refine
Example: Analyzing the 2025 Pandemic Agreement in 2026 Context

The Situation: You need to understand how the 2025 WHO Pandemic Agreement affects your country's obligations, but implementation varies globally due to differing ratification timelines and participation levels.

AI-Assisted Approach:

  1. Use AI to analyze the agreement text: Ask AI to summarize key provisions relevant to your country's context
  2. Verify with official sources: Check WHO's official Pandemic Agreement page for ratification status and official interpretations
  3. Regional context: Use AI to compare approaches across your region, then verify with regional health organization updates
  4. Implementation planning: Use AI to draft implementation options, then consult with national health authorities
  5. Monitor developments: Use AI with web search capability (like Perplexity) to track ongoing developments and discussions

Key Principle: AI is a powerful analytical tool, but in the current complex international environment, human judgment, official sources, and direct communication with relevant authorities remain essential for decision-making.

Limitations and Ethical Considerations

Important Considerations When Using AI in Health Security:

  • Data Quality and Biases: AI systems are only as good as the data they're trained on. Biased or incomplete data can lead to flawed analyses or recommendations.
  • Privacy and Security: The use of AI in health surveillance must balance public health needs with privacy rights and data protection.
  • Verification and Validation: AI outputs should be verified by human experts, especially for critical health security decisions.
  • Equity and Access: Ensure AI implementation doesn't exacerbate existing health disparities between populations.
  • Transparency: Decision-makers should understand how AI-generated recommendations are produced.
