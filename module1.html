Module 1: Introduction to Generative AI

Learning Objectives

  • Understand what generative artificial intelligence is and how it differs from traditional AI
  • Recognize the key capabilities and limitations of modern AI systems
  • Identify potential applications of generative AI in global health security contexts

What is Generative Artificial Intelligence?

Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to AI systems that can create new content, including text, images, audio, and video, that resembles human-created content. These systems are "generative" because they generate new outputs rather than simply classifying or analyzing existing data.

Modern generative AI models like Claude, ChatGPT, and others are built on a type of AI architecture called Large Language Models (LLMs). These are neural networks trained on vast amounts of text data from the internet, books, articles, and other sources, allowing them to:

  • Generate human-like text responses to prompts
  • Understand context and follow instructions
  • Perform a wide range of language-based tasks
  • Adapt to different styles, formats, and specialized domains

How Generative AI Works

At a fundamental level, generative AI models like LLMs work by:

  1. Training: Learning patterns from massive datasets
  2. Pattern recognition: Identifying relationships between words, concepts, and information
  3. Prediction: Generating new content by predicting what would likely come next in a sequence

These models don't "understand" information the way humans do, but they can produce remarkably useful and accurate responses based on statistical patterns they've learned. Frontier LLMs are also trained to work with images and videos.

Example: How an LLM Might Complete This Sequence

Input: "The World Health Organization declared a Public Health Emergency of International Concern in response to..."

Possible AI completion: "...the outbreak of COVID-19 on January 30, 2020, recognizing the novel coronavirus as a significant risk to multiple countries and requiring a coordinated international response."

Real-World Applications: 2025-2026 Examples

Example 1: Analyzing the 2025 WHO Pandemic Agreement

The Challenge: The WHO Pandemic Agreement was adopted in 2025 after years of negotiation. Health security professionals need to understand its implications for their countries and organizations.

How AI Can Help:

  • Quick Summary: Ask AI to summarize the key provisions in plain language
  • Comparative Analysis: Request a comparison between the Pandemic Agreement and the 2024 IHR amendments
  • Impact Assessment: Generate frameworks for analyzing the agreement's impact on specific regions or health systems
  • Implementation Planning: Create draft action plans for implementing agreement provisions

Example AI Prompt:

"Summarize the key obligations for Member States under the 2025 WHO Pandemic Agreement, focusing on: (1) surveillance and early warning systems, (2) equitable access to medical countermeasures, and (3) financing mechanisms. For each area, explain what actions countries need to take."

Important Note: Always verify AI-generated information about international agreements against official WHO sources. AI is excellent for initial analysis but should not replace reading the actual agreement text.

Example 2: Learning from the Mpox Multi-Country Outbreak (2022-2025)

The Situation: Beginning in 2022, mpox spread to over 100 countries in an unprecedented multi-country outbreak, declared a PHEIC in July 2022. The outbreak highlighted gaps in surveillance, vaccine equity, and stigma management.

How AI Helps Analyze This Case:

1. Synthesizing Lessons Learned

AI can quickly summarize key findings from multiple outbreak reports, WHO updates, and peer-reviewed studies to identify common themes and recommendations.

2. Comparative Analysis

Compare response approaches across different countries or regions to identify successful strategies and challenges.

3. Developing Future Protocols

Use lessons from mpox to create improved response protocols for similar scenarios.

Example AI Prompt:

"Based on the mpox multi-country outbreak (2022-2025), identify the three most critical gaps in the international response and suggest specific improvements for future similar outbreaks. Focus on: surveillance systems, vaccine distribution, and community engagement. Format your response as actionable recommendations for health security planners."

Key Lessons from Mpox Relevant to AI Use:

  • AI can help identify surveillance gaps by analyzing outbreak data patterns
  • Rapid information synthesis aids in adapting response strategies as outbreaks evolve
  • AI can assist in developing culturally appropriate communication materials for affected communities
  • Pattern recognition can help predict which populations may be at higher risk

Limitations and Considerations

Important Limitations to Be Aware Of:

  • Information Cutoff: AI models have a knowledge cutoff date and lack information about very recent events
  • Hallucinations: AI can sometimes generate incorrect information that sounds plausible
  • Lack of True Understanding: AI doesn't truly comprehend concepts the way humans do
  • Limited Critical Thinking: AI can't make ethical judgments or assess the real-world impact of decisions
  • Security and Privacy Concerns: Information shared with public AI tools may not be private

Case Study: AI in Recent Health Security Events

Mpox Outbreak Response (2022-2025)

During the mpox multi-country outbreak, health officials needed to:

  • Rapidly update case definitions and testing criteria
  • Coordinate international vaccine distribution
  • Address misinformation and stigma
  • Adapt surveillance systems for new transmission patterns

AI Applications:

  • Analyzing social media for early outbreak signals
  • Generating tailored risk communication materials for different communities
  • Synthesizing evolving clinical guidance from multiple sources
  • Creating training materials for healthcare workers

H5N1 Monitoring (Ongoing)

The persistent threat of H5N1 influenza requires continuous:

  • Monitoring of avian-to-human transmission events
  • Analysis of viral evolution patterns
  • Assessment of pandemic potential
  • Preparedness planning updates

AI Applications:

  • Tracking and summarizing global surveillance data
  • Comparing current strain characteristics with historical data
  • Generating scenario-based preparedness plans
  • Analyzing gaps in current response capacities

Why These Examples Matter for This Course:

These real-world cases demonstrate that AI is not just a theoretical tool—it's already being used in health security responses. Understanding these applications will help you:

  • Recognize opportunities to use AI in your own work
  • Understand both the potential and limitations of AI in crisis situations
  • Learn from how others have successfully (and unsuccessfully) applied AI
  • Prepare for using AI tools in the GCSP course modules on outbreak response, IHR, and international collaboration
